AdvertisementSupported byBy The Associated PressStocks experienced their biggest loss in a month-and-a-half on Thursday as shares of banks and technology companies tumbled. Equities have fallen three out of four days this week.Treasury rates moved lower, hurting financial stocks, as the dollar continued to fall against the Japanese yen.Stocks sharply reversed course after their gains a day earlier. Financial companies including Goldman Sachs and Citigroup took the largest losses. Technology and telecommunications companies also fell.The market has lost momentum over the last few weeks after a furious rally that wiped out most of its losses from early this year. Scott Wren, senior global equity strategist for Wells Fargo’s Investment Institute, said stocks were rising and falling based on how investors expect the global economy to do.“People are worried about growth today,” he said. “You’re not getting much more than modest economic activity.”The Standard & Poor’s 500-stock index shed 24.75 points, or 1.2 percent, to 2,041.91. The Nasdaq composite index lost 72.35 points, or 1.5 percent, to 4,848.37.The Dow Jones industrial average fell 174.09 points, or 1 percent, to 17,541.96.Financial companies fell sharply. Goldman Sachs slid $4.78, or 3.1 percent, to $150.41, and Citigroup lost $1.59, or 3.8 percent, to $40.27, while JPMorgan Chase dropped $1.49, or 2.5 percent, to $57.32.Mr. Wren said banks were struggling because economic growth was sluggish and rates remained low, which means the banks cannot make as much money from lending. “Interest rates aren’t going to do what banks really need them to do,” he said.EBay led tech stocks lower as it fell $1.33, or 5.2 percent, to $24.10, while Apple gave up $2.42, or 2.2 percent, to $108.54. Telecommunications companies also continued to struggle. Verizon fell $1.52, or 2.8 percent, to $52.Wynn Resorts jumped $10.44, or 11.7 percent, to $99.99 after it announced a new development. The company said it hoped to build a recreational lake and hotel behind its Wynn Las Vegas property.Costco Wholesale Corporation declined after its March sales missed estimates. Its stock fell $4.74, or 3 percent, to $152.03.Ollie’s Bargain Outlet Holdings traded higher after it announced solid quarterly results. The stock soared $2.34, or 10.3 percent, to $25.04.HanesBrands said it would buy Champion Europe. HanesBrands already owns the Champion brand in the Americas, Asia and Australia, and the deal gives it control of a company that owns the Champion trademark in Europe as well as the Middle East and Africa. It recently made a similar deal with its Japan unit. Shares of HanesBrands, an underwear, T-shirt and sock maker, added 77 cents, or 2.8 percent, to $27.87.ConAgra Foods added 66 cents, or 1.4 percent, to $46.09. The company, maker of Chef Boyardee, Hebrew National hot dogs and other packaged foods, reported third-quarter profit and sales were stronger than expected.The used car dealership chain CarMax reported strong quarterly results, but its stock lost $3.81, or 7.1 percent, to $49.48. The company said it had faced a tougher sales environment in the previous two quartersThe Labor Department said applications for unemployment benefits fell slightly last week. The figures showed that employers are not cutting jobs, even though economic growth remains tenuous.United States government bond prices rose. The yield on the 10-year Treasury note fell to 1.69 percent from 1.76 percent.The dollar continued to weaken against the yen, and is now at its lowest against the yen in almost a year and a half. On Wednesday it fell to 108.36 yen from 109.74 yen. The euro declined to $1.1370 from $1.1398.The price of gold rose $13.70 or 1.1 percent,to settle at $1,237.50 an ounce and silver gained 10 cents to $15.16 an ounce. Copper plunged seven cents, or 3.1 percent, to $2.08 a pound.Benchmark American crude fell 49 cents, or 1.3 percent, to $37.26 a barrel in New York. Brent crude, used to price international oils, dropped 41 cents to trade at $39.43 a barrel in London.In other energy trading, wholesale gasoline fell 1 cent to $1.38 a gallon. Heating oil slipped 1 cent to $1.13 a gallon. Natural gas rose 11 cents, or 5.6 percent, to $2.02 per 1,000 cubic feet.Germany’s DAX fell 1 percent and the CAC-40 in France shed 0.9 percent. Britain’s FTSE 100 lost 0.4 percent. In Tokyo, the Nikkei 225 edged up 0.2 percent and Hong Kong’s Hang Seng added 0.3 percent. In South Korea, the Kospi was barely changed, rising 0.1 percent.Advertisement

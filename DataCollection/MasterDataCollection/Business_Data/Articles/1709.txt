Stocks on Wall Street resumed their climb on Monday as investors bought shares that stand to benefit from economic growth, like those of banks, as well as technology companies, which have mostly been left out of a postelection rally. The Dow Jones industrial average set another record high.Energy company stocks rose as the price of oil reached its highest level since July 2015. Small-company stocks continued to outpace the rest of the market.Technology companies have fallen since the election as big names like Facebook and Alphabet have lost ground, but that changed on Monday.The Dow Jones industrial average rose 45.82 points, or 0.2 percent, to 19,216.24 and went as high as 19,274. The Standard & Poor’s 500-stock index climbed 12.76 points, or 0.6 percent, to 2,204.71. The Nasdaq composite added 53.24 points, or 1 percent, to 5,308.89.AdvertisementStocks of small and midsize companies rose sharply. The Russell 2000 index jumped 23.53 points, or 1.8 percent, to 1,337.79. Thanks to a big rally in November, the Russell is up 18 percent this year, more than twice as much as the S.&P. 500, which tracks large United States companies.AdvertisementBanks resumed their postelection rally, trading at their highest levels since early 2008. Goldman Sachs gained $5.19, or 2.3 percent, to $228.55, a nine-year high. While stocks traded lower over all last week, banks are on a four-week winning streak since the election.Microsoft added 97 cents, or 1.6 percent, to $60.22. Salesforce, the customer-management software developer, climbed $2.39, or 3.5 percent, to $70.80. Tech stocks are down about 1 percent since the election as investors wonder about the effects of the potential trade policies of President-elect Donald J. Trump.

Oil prices rose for the fourth day in a row. The price of oil has surged since the Organization of the Petroleum Exporting Countries completed a deal that will trim oil production starting in January. Benchmark United States oil rose 11 cents, to $51.79 per barrel, in New York. Brent crude, used to price international oils, gained 48 cents, to $54.94 a barrel, in London.That sent energy companies higher. Valero Energy gained $3.06, or 5 percent, to $64.52, and ConocoPhillips picked up 76 cents, or 1.6 percent, to $48.88.Consumer-focused companies also did better than the rest of the market.
